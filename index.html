What is Classical Chinese Medicine (CCM)?

Classical Chinese Medicine (CCM) draws directly from early source texts—especially the Shānghánzábìnglùn (《伤寒杂病论》) and Jīng Guì Yào Lüè (《金匮要略》). Rather than modern standardized “organ patterns,” CCM reads the body through movement states of qi and fluids across layers (exterior ↔ interior), qualities (cold ↔ heat), and dynamics (repletion ↔ vacuity, pivoting, and counterflow).

CCM vs TCM—what’s different?

  • Map: CCM prioritizes the classical frameworks (e.g., the Six Conformations / 六经). TCM often uses later doctrinal systems and standardized patterns.
  • State over label: CCM emphasizes state transitions (e.g., Taiyang → Shaoyang → Yangming) rather than fixed labels.
  • Therapeutic intent: Interventions follow the current conformation state (release the surface, free the pivot, drain/replenish the interior, warm the core, harmonize reversal).
  • For you (the user): Results point to which conformation to regulate today; this is not a disease diagnosis.

Six Conformations vs TCM “Patterns”

The Six Conformations—Taiyang, Yangming, Shaoyang, Taiyin, Shaoyin, Jueyin—are positions/trajectories of pathology (surface/interior, hot/cold, repletion/vacuity, reversal). TCM “patterns” (证型) are named aggregates like “Liver Qi Stagnation” or “Spleen Qi Deficiency.” This self-check uses the Six Conformations because they are time-sensitive, action-oriented, and align with classical therapeutic logic: release, pivot, drain, warm, harmonize.
